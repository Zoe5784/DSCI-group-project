Introduction:

Professors frequently provide guidelines and benchmarks for enhancing students academic performances and evaluating student knowledge assessments. The process of determining students' knowledge and comprehension of a specific subject is known as student knowledge assessment. It aids in identifying areas where students require further support or challenge, along with evaluating instructional efficacy, making crucial decisions such as student placement and curriculum development, and monitoring educational quality. Furthermore, the student knowledge assessment is critical for measuring student development, influencing instruction, and offering feedback to improve student performance and the entire teaching and learning experience.

This research paper aims to utilize classification KNN algorithms to evaluate student performance and knowledge during their education. The model applies a database from the UCI Machine Learning Repository called the User Knowledge Model (Kahraman et al., 2013). It collected an actual dataset on the students' knowledge levels in the field of electrical DC machines. The dataset of 403 samples assessed three of the students' study habits and three learning outcomes: 1. STG (study time with new materials). 2. SCG (number of repeat users for new materials). 3. STR (the user's study time for related objects using fresh materials). 4. LPR (Users' exam performance for linked objects and a goal object) 5. PEG (The exam performance of users for goal objects) 6. UNS (The knowledge level of users).

In terms of response variables, the model will include STG, SCG, STR. According to the research, study time improves people's comprehension of study material (Masui et al., 2014). Furthermore, Zhan et al. (2018) found that the frequency of repetition of study content improves people's knowledge of it. Additionally, Bloom's taxonomy hightlighted a hierarchical structure for the learning process that has been revised with six domains in which remembering (retrieving relevant knowledge) is the initial phase, implying that study-related material is a crucial factor in developing the model (Secret et al., 2017). As exam performance for goal objects and linked objects, and the knowledge level of users evaluate the same subject, which is the student knowledge assessment, we will only take UNS as explanatory variable.

Overall, our research was motivated by the need for more systematic and detailed investigations that assess the prediction of a user's relative knowledge level based on the degree of their study habits. The study will focus on how to predict a user's knowledge level so that course content can be tailored accordingly.

We will perform a 5-fold cross-validation on our new training set, while adjusting the K values from 2 to 10 to find the most accurate model. Although the predictors appear to be scaled we will do this once again ourselves for safety.

In[7]:

options(repr.plot.height = 5, repr.plot.width = 6)

user_recipe <- recipe(UNS ~ ., data = user_training) |>
                step_scale(all_predictors()) |>
                step_center(all_predictors())

user_spec <- nearest_neighbor(weight_func = "rectangular", neighbors = tune()) |>
                set_engine("kknn") |>
                set_mode("classification")

user_vfold <- vfold_cv(user_training, v = 5, strata = UNS)

user_workflow <- workflow() |>
                add_recipe(user_recipe) |>
                add_model(user_spec)

gridvals <- tibble(neighbors = seq(from =2, to = 10, by = 1))

user_results <- user_workflow |>
                tune_grid(resamples = user_vfold, grid = gridvals) |>

accuracies <- user_results |>

Discussion:

Our findings suggest there is a relatively weak correlation between the study habits weve chosen to analyze and a users knowledge level. Our KNN algorithm reported at most ~43% of the users knowledge levels correctly when performing on our training data (Figure 2), and reported a similar 42.7% accuracy when performing on our testing data (Table 2). This outcome does in fact reflect a large overlap we observed in many knowledge levels based on visual analysis in both our previous proposal and earlier summary (Figure 1). Both indicate that the study habits which weve chosen to use are insufficient at confidently predicting an individual's general understanding of a topic. These findings could highlight a limitation of using study metrics to represent the true degree to which students comprehend a particular topic. Encouraging further studies to explore aspects of students unrelated to studying. For example; additional course load, hours of sleep, interest in course material, or satisfaction with course experience. Analyzing factors beyond study habits may provide better insight into how a student understands course materials, and how their out of class habits could help provide feedback for future implementation and revision of course content.
